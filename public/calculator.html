Frequently Asked Questions

What is the carnivore diet?

The carnivore diet is a meat-based eating approach that focuses exclusively on animal products: beef, pork, chicken, fish, eggs, and some dairy. It eliminates all plant foods including vegetables, fruits, grains, and legumes. Followers eat nose-to-tail, prioritizing nutrient-dense organ meats alongside muscle meat.

How much protein do I need on carnivore?

Protein needs on carnivore vary by goal and activity level. For fat loss, aim for 1.0-1.2g per pound of lean body mass. For muscle gain, target 1.2-1.6g per pound. For maintenance, 0.8-1.0g per pound is sufficient. Most people undereat protein by 20-30g daily, which can cause fatigue and stalls. Use our calculator to find your personalized protein target.

How do I calculate carnivore macros?

To calculate carnivore macros: (1) Determine your lean body mass by subtracting body fat from total weight. (2) Set protein based on your goal (fat loss, muscle gain, or maintenance). (3) Calculate remaining calories from fat. (4) Adjust for activity level. Our free calculator does this automatically in 90 seconds based on your stats, goal, and activity level.

Do I need to track calories on carnivore?

Not everyone needs to track calories on carnivore. Many people eat to satiety and lose fat naturally. However, tracking helps if: (1) You've stalled after 3+ months, (2) You're trying to gain muscle, (3) You're exhausted or losing too much weight, or (4) You're not seeing expected results. Knowing your macro targets removes guesswork and helps optimize results.

What's the difference between carnivore and keto?

Carnivore is a subset of keto but more restrictive. Keto allows 20-50g carbs from any source (vegetables, nuts, low-carb foods). Carnivore eliminates all plant foods and focuses exclusively on animal products, resulting in near-zero carbs. Carnivore prioritizes protein (1.0-1.6g per lb), while keto is often moderate protein. Both achieve ketosis, but carnivore is simpler: just meat, eggs, and animal products.
