The Anti-Resolution Playbook: Why Goals Beat Resolutions Every Time

New Year's resolutions are trash. Let me be direct about that.

They're designed to fail. You wake up January 1st with explosive motivation, you set some vague goal like "eat healthier" or "lose weight," you crush it for a week, then by January 10th you're back to your old habits wondering what happened.

That's not you failing. That's the system being broken.

Here's what actually works instead.

Resolutions vs. Goals: The Fundamental Difference

A resolution is a decision. A goal is a target with a plan.

Resolution: "I'm going to stick to carnivore in 2025."

Goal: "I'm eating only meat and salt for 30 days. After 30 days, I'll measure energy levels and body composition. If metrics improve, I continue. If not, I adjust."

One is hope. One is a system.

The difference? Goals are specific, measurable, and connected to reality. Resolutions are wishes.

The Problem with "All or Nothing" Thinking

Most people who fail at carnivore do it because they think one slip-up means failure.

You have a birthday party. Someone offers you cake. You eat a slice. Suddenly you think the whole thing is ruined, so you eat a whole cake, then resume "perfectly" next Monday.

That's not how results work.

Real life is 95% compliance, not 100%. You miss meals. You get pressured at dinners. You travel. Life happens.

A real goal accounts for this. A resolution ignores it and then punishes you when reality shows up.

The Three-Layer Goal System That Actually Works

Layer 1: The Big Goal (the why)

Not "lose weight." Why do you want that? More energy? Better sleep? Look better in photos? Run without getting tired? Be present for your kids?

Pick one. Write it down. Make it visceral. Don't let it be abstract.

Layer 2: The Quarterly Target (the measurable)

In 90 days, what specifically changes? Weight down 15 pounds? Energy up? Sleeping through the night? No afternoon crashes?

Something you can actually measure in three months, not something vague.

Layer 3: The Weekly Protocol (the daily)

This is the thing you actually control. Not "be healthier." But "eat meat and salt. No processed food. Check in on Sundays about how I feel."

The daily action is what moves the needle. The goal just makes sure you know why you're doing the daily action.

The Math Behind Consistency

You don't need perfection. You need consistency.

If you stick to carnivore 80% of the time over a year, you still see massive results. Most people see results at 70% compliance.

That means you can miss meals. You can have slip-ups. You can be human.

What kills progress is the all-or-nothing collapse. One cheat meal becomes a cheat week becomes a cheat month, and then it's February and you're back to zero.

If you build a goal that expects 80% compliance, you win. If you build a resolution that demands 100%, you lose.

The Accountability Hack

Most people set goals in their head and wonder why they fail.

Write them down. Specific. Measurable. With dates.

"In 90 days (April 1), I will have stable energy throughout the day (no 3pm crashes), sleep through the night, and weigh 185 pounds. My weekly protocol is meat, salt, water, nothing else. I check in every Sunday."

That's not a resolution. That's a plan with teeth.

Share it with someone. Not to brag. To be accountable. Text a friend every Sunday with your status. Sounds simple. It works.

Why Carnivore Is Your Competitive Advantage

Here's the thing about eating only meat.

Most diets require constant willpower. You're always deciding what to eat. Carnivore removes that. Your decision tree shrinks to one choice: am I eating meat right now?

That's not restriction. That's freedom from decision fatigue.

A goal on carnivore is easier to stick to than a goal on any other diet, because you're not battling yourself every meal.

Use that advantage. Build your goal around it.

The 30-Day Reset

You don't need a full year to see if carnivore works for you.

30 days. Meat and salt, nothing else. Measure three things: energy, sleep, weight.

After 30 days, you'll know. Not guess. Know.

If it works, great. You just built your 90-day goal on solid data. If it doesn't, you didn't waste a year wondering.

Most people feel dramatically different by day 30. Some take longer. But 30 days is enough data to decide if you're in or out.

The Real Anti-Resolution

Don't promise yourself you'll be perfect in 2025.

Promise yourself you'll be consistent. That you'll measure results. That when you slip up, you don't collapse, you just keep going.

Promise yourself you'll actually do the thing instead of just thinking about it.

That's not a resolution. That's a commitment.

And commitments stick.
