Bone Broth: Why It's a Carnivore Staple

Health coach and science writer focused on metabolic health and evidence-based nutrition. 8+ years researching how food affects our bodies.

TL;DR

  • Bone broth provides collagen, glycine, and minerals in a form your body actually absorbs well.
  • Research supports benefits for gut health, joint support, and sleep quality.
  • Homemade is better than store-bought. Simmer bones 12-24 hours with a splash of vinegar.
  • Start with 1 cup daily and see how you feel after two weeks.

Bone broth isn't just a trend. People have been simmering bones for thousands of years. Our great-grandmothers knew something we forgot: you don't throw away the bones. You extract every bit of nutrition from them.

Here's what the research actually shows about bone broth, and why it's become a staple in the carnivore community.

What's Actually in Bone Broth?

When you simmer bones for hours, you're extracting compounds that are hard to get elsewhere in the modern diet:

Collagen & Gelatin

Collagen breaks down into gelatin during cooking. This provides the amino acids glycine and proline, which support skin, hair, nails, and connective tissue. Your body uses these to build its own collagen.

Glycine

Glycine is a conditionally essential amino acid. Most people don't get enough. It supports liver detox, sleep quality, and helps balance out the methionine from muscle meat. Research shows glycine supplementation improves sleep.

Minerals

Calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, and potassium leach from the bones into the broth. The amounts vary based on cooking time and the bones you use, but they're in a form that's easy to absorb.

Glucosamine & Chondroitin

These compounds come from cartilage and connective tissue. They're the same ingredients in joint supplements, but in whole-food form. Research shows they support joint health.

The beauty of bone broth is that it delivers nutrients in combination, the way nature intended. You're not isolated supplements. You're getting the whole package.

What the Research Shows

Let me be clear about the evidence levels here. Bone broth specifically hasn't been studied in large clinical trials. But the individual components have solid research behind them.

Evidence by Benefit

Strong Glycine improves sleep quality in human trials
Strong Collagen peptides support skin elasticity and joint comfort
Moderate Glucosamine and chondroitin for joint health
Moderate Gelatin supports gut lining integrity

Gut Health: Here's What I've Seen Work

Many carnivore folks report improved digestion when they add bone broth. The gelatin in bone broth may help strengthen the gut lining. This is particularly relevant if you're dealing with digestive issues or transitioning to carnivore.

The research on gelatin and gut health is promising but not conclusive. What we know: gelatin can help restore the mucus layer in the gut, and glycine has anti-inflammatory effects. If your gut is struggling, bone broth is a gentle way to support healing.

I've worked with people who found bone broth essential during their carnivore transition. Others don't notice much difference. Your situation might be different.

Joint Support

This one has decent evidence. The glucosamine and chondroitin in bone broth are the same compounds used in joint supplements. When you simmer bones with cartilage and connective tissue attached, you're extracting these naturally.

Research shows collagen supplementation can reduce joint pain and improve mobility, especially in active people. Bone broth provides collagen in a bioavailable form, along with the cofactors that help your body use it.

Sleep Quality

Glycine is the sleeper hit here. Studies show that 3 grams of glycine before bed improves sleep quality and reduces daytime fatigue. A cup of bone broth provides roughly 2-3 grams of glycine, depending on how it's made.

If you're struggling with sleep on carnivore, a cup of bone broth in the evening is worth trying. It's not a sedative. It just helps your body relax and fall into deeper sleep.

How to Make Good Bone Broth

Basic Protocol

1 Use bones with connective tissue attached. Beef knuckles, oxtails, chicken feet, and marrow bones all work well. Mix them for variety.
2 Add a splash of apple cider vinegar (2 tablespoons per quart of water). This helps extract minerals from the bones.
3 Cover with cold water and bring to a simmer. Don't boil it hard. Keep it at a gentle simmer.
4 Simmer for 12-24 hours for beef bones, 8-12 hours for chicken. Longer = more extraction.
5 Strain, cool, and store. Good broth gels when refrigerated. That's the collagen.

The gel test is real. If your broth jiggles like jello when cold, you've extracted good collagen. If it stays liquid, you either didn't cook long enough or didn't use enough connective tissue.

Store-Bought vs. Homemade

Homemade is better. Most store-bought bone broth is cooked for shorter times and doesn't contain much actual collagen. Check the protein content. Good bone broth should have 7-10 grams of protein per cup. Many commercial versions have 2-3 grams.

If you can't make your own, look for brands that specify long cook times and show higher protein content. Some people find powdered collagen peptides more convenient. These work too, but you miss the minerals and the whole-food synergy.

Who Benefits Most

Most Likely to Notice Benefits

  • People with joint pain or stiffness
  • People transitioning to carnivore with digestive issues
  • People with sleep problems
  • Athletes and active people putting stress on joints
  • Anyone eating mostly muscle meat without organs

If you're eating nose-to-tail with lots of organ meats and connective tissue, you might already be getting plenty of these nutrients. But for people eating mainly steaks and ground beef, bone broth fills in gaps.

How to Start

Begin with one cup daily, either in the morning or evening. Some people sip it plain. Others use it as a base for cooking. Give it two weeks before deciding if it's helping.

Track what you notice: sleep quality, joint comfort, digestion, energy levels. Everyone responds differently. The only way to know if it helps you is to try it consistently and pay attention.

Key Takeaways

  1. Bone broth provides collagen, glycine, minerals, and glucosamine in a bioavailable form.
  2. Research supports benefits for sleep (glycine), joints (collagen, glucosamine), and potentially gut health.
  3. Homemade is significantly better than store-bought. Look for the "gel test" when cold.
  4. One cup daily for two weeks is a reasonable trial period to see if you notice benefits.
