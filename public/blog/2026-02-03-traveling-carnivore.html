The Traveling Carnivore: How to Eat Clean on the Road

by sarah

Why Travel Breaks Most Diets

Travel disrupts everything. Your routine, your kitchen, your control over food choices. Most people give up on their diet the moment they book a flight.

Carnivore is actually easier to maintain while traveling than most diets, but you need a system. Here's what works based on research and real experience from people who travel frequently on this protocol.

The Airport Problem

Airports are designed to sell you processed carbs. But there are always options if you know where to look.

Before Security: If there's time, hit a grocery store near the airport. Grab hard-boiled eggs, pre-cooked chicken, beef sticks, cheese if you tolerate it. Pack it in your carry-on. TSA allows solid food.

Past Security: Most airports have at least one sit-down restaurant with real food. Look for:

Burger places - Order bunless burgers. Add extra patties if you're hungry. Most places accommodate this without issue.

Steakhouses - Expensive, but you get actual meat cooked properly. Steak and eggs works at any time of day.

Breakfast spots - Eggs, bacon, sausage. Skip the toast and hash browns. Simple.

Packaged Options: If you're stuck with grab-and-go, look for beef jerky (check ingredients - many have sugar), pork rinds, packaged hard-boiled eggs, or smoked salmon. Not ideal, but better than starving or eating a bagel.

Flying Long-Haul

Flights over 6 hours require planning. You can't rely on airplane food unless you're in business class and can pre-order something specific.

Pack Your Own: Cooked steak or chicken breast in a container. Hard-boiled eggs. Beef sticks. All of these make it through security fine.

Pre-Flight Meal: Eat a large meal before you board. If you're full going into the flight, you can skip the airline meal entirely and just sleep.

Hydration and Electrolytes: Bring electrolyte packets (LMNT or similar). Buy water after security. Flying dehydrates you, and if you're already adapting to carnivore, you need extra electrolytes anyway.

Hotel Stays

Hotels vary wildly. Some have full kitchens, others have nothing but a coffee maker.

Full Kitchen: Hit a local grocery store immediately. Stock up on ground beef, eggs, bacon. Cook your own meals. This is the gold standard for longer stays.

Mini-Fridge Only: Pre-cooked rotisserie chicken, hard-boiled eggs, deli meat, cheese (if you eat it). You can survive a few days on this without cooking.

No Fridge: Tougher, but doable. Beef sticks, canned fish (sardines, tuna, salmon), pork rinds. Not gourmet, but functional.

Hotel Breakfast Buffets: Usually have eggs, bacon, sausage. Skip the waffles and pastries. Load up on protein. If the quality looks questionable, eat what you can and supplement with food you brought.

Road Trips

Road trips are the easiest travel scenario for carnivore. You control the stops, you can pack a cooler, and there are fast food options everywhere.

Pack a Cooler: Pre-cooked meat, hard-boiled eggs, cheese, butter. Keep it on ice. You can go 1-2 days before needing to restock ice.

Fast Food Strategy: Burgers without buns are available everywhere. Ask for extra patties, add bacon, skip the vegetables if you're strict. McDonald's, Burger King, Wendy's, Five Guys - all work.

Sit-Down Chains: Steakhouses (Texas Roadhouse, Outback, etc.) are in every town. Steak, no sides. Easy.

Gas Station Stops: Most have hard-boiled eggs, beef jerky (check labels), cheese sticks, sometimes rotisserie taquitos (check ingredients). Not great, but options exist.

International Travel

Different countries, different challenges. But meat is universal.

Europe: Excellent meat quality. Supermarkets have great options. Restaurants understand "just the meat, no sauce." Language barriers are minimal for simple requests.

Asia: Harder. Rice and noodles dominate. Look for Korean BBQ, Japanese yakiniku, hotpot restaurants where you control what goes in. Street food is risky unless it's obviously just grilled meat.

Latin America: Meat culture is strong. Argentina, Brazil, Mexico - all easy. Steakhouses everywhere, quality is usually excellent. Just avoid sauces and sides.

General Rule: Learn how to say "just meat, no sauce, no vegetables" in the local language. Most servers understand dietary restrictions if you're clear.

Eating Out: Restaurant Strategies

Whether you're traveling or just eating out at home, these tactics work anywhere.

Call Ahead: If it's a nicer restaurant, call and explain your needs. Most kitchens can accommodate "steak and eggs, nothing else" if they know in advance.

Order Confidently: Don't apologize or over-explain. "Ribeye, medium-rare, no sides" is a complete order. If they push sides, say "no thank you" and move on.

Avoid Hidden Ingredients: Ask about marinades, sauces, butter blends (some restaurants use vegetable oil in their "butter"). If you're strict, specify cooking in animal fat only or dry-cooked.

Steakhouses Are Your Friend: They understand meat. They won't question your order. The quality is usually good. It's expensive, but reliable.

BBQ Joints: Good option if they cook with wood or charcoal. Watch out for sugar-heavy sauces and rubs. Ask for unsauced meat if possible.

Business Travel

Business travel adds social pressure. Client dinners, conferences, networking events where food is social currency.

Client Dinners: Pick the restaurant if you can. Steakhouse makes it easy. If you can't pick, review the menu beforehand and know what you can order.

Conference Meals: Usually terrible. Eat beforehand. Bring backup food (beef sticks, hard-boiled eggs). Pick at what's available without making a scene.

Networking Events: Eat before you go. If there's food, look for meat and cheese trays, shrimp cocktail (skip the sauce), anything protein-based. You can hold a plate and network without actually eating much.

Don't Make It Weird: People notice when you make a big deal about your diet. Order simply, don't explain unless asked, and keep the focus on the business at hand.

What to Pack (Checklist)

For any trip, these items make life easier:

Beef sticks or jerky (read labels - many have sugar)
Hard-boiled eggs (if traveling by car)
Electrolyte packets (LMNT, Redmond Re-Lyte, or similar)
Small container of salt
Pre-cooked meat (if you have a cooler)
Canned fish (sardines, salmon, tuna) - TSA-approved
Pork rinds (if you eat them)

Optional: Small cutting board and knife if staying somewhere with a kitchen. Ziplock bags for leftovers.

Managing Adaptation While Traveling

If you're new to carnivore (less than 60 days in), traveling is harder. Your body is still adapting, and stress + travel can make symptoms worse.

Electrolytes Are Critical: You're already losing electrolytes during adaptation. Add travel stress and dehydration, and you'll feel terrible. Pack electrolytes and use them daily.

Digestion May Get Weird: Travel + new foods + stress can mess with digestion. Don't panic. Stick to familiar meats if possible. Avoid experimenting with new cuts or types of meat while traveling.

Energy May Dip: Sleep disruption + time zones + dietary changes can tank your energy. Plan lighter days if possible. Don't schedule important meetings right after a red-eye flight.

When You Can't Find Good Options

Sometimes you're stuck. Rural areas with limited food options, countries where meat quality is questionable, situations where you just can't get what you need.

Prioritize Protein: Even if it's not ideal meat, eating some protein is better than eating carbs and feeling terrible.

Fast if Necessary: If the only options are processed junk, just don't eat. You won't die from skipping a meal. Once you're fat-adapted, going 24 hours without food is manageable.

Don't Catastrophize: One imperfect meal won't ruin your progress. Eat what you can, get back on track at the next meal, move on.

Long-Term Travel (Weeks or Months)

Extended travel requires more infrastructure.

Find Local Butchers: Grocery store meat varies by country. Local butchers usually have better quality and can cut meat to your specs.

Set Up a Routine: Find 2-3 reliable restaurants or food sources and rotate them. Trying new places constantly is exhausting. Build a routine that works.

Connect with Locals: Expat groups, online forums, carnivore communities - someone has already figured out where to get good meat in whatever city you're in. Ask.

Adjust Expectations: Meat quality and availability vary. Grass-fed might not be an option. Organ meats might be more available than muscle meat. Be flexible.

The Mental Game

Travel is stressful. Adding dietary restrictions makes it more stressful. Here's how to manage the mental side.

Plan Ahead: Reduce decisions by planning meals in advance. Knowing where you'll eat and what you'll order eliminates daily decision fatigue.

Lower Your Standards Temporarily: You're not going to eat perfectly while traveling. That's fine. Focus on protein and staying off carbs, not on finding the perfect grass-fed ribeye.

Give Yourself Grace: If you slip up, it's not the end of the world. Get back on track at the next meal. Don't spiral into "I already messed up so I might as well eat pizza."

What Actually Matters

Traveling carnivore is doable. It requires more planning than traveling Standard American Diet, but it's not impossible.

Key principles: prioritize protein, pack backup food, know your options before you're starving, and don't stress about perfection.

The goal is to maintain your protocol well enough that you don't lose progress, without making travel miserable. Find the balance that works for you.
