The Animal-Based Debate: Is Paul Saladino Right About Honey and Fruit?

And you're wondering: Did he betray the movement? Or did he figure something out that strict carnivores are missing?

What Paul Saladino Actually Says

1

Carbs aren't inherently bad

The problem with the Standard American Diet isn't carbs‚Äîit's industrial foods. Honey and fruit are "clean" carbs that humans have eaten for thousands of years.

2

Active people perform better with carbs

Athletes, people who do intense exercise, and people with high stress might perform better with some glucose. Honey and fruit provide that without the downsides of grains.

3

Electrolyte balance is easier with carbs

Zero-carb carnivore can cause low aldosterone, which messes with sodium retention. Adding carbs helps your body hold onto electrolytes better.

"The debate isn't really about science. It's about individual response. Some people thrive on zero-carb. Some people do better with a little fruit. Both can work."

What Strict Carnivores Say

1

You don't need carbs‚Äîhumans are fat-adapted

Once you're fat-adapted (usually 3-6 months in), your body runs on fat and ketones. Your liver makes what little glucose you need via gluconeogenesis.

2

Honey and fruit are still sugar

Fructose from fruit goes straight to your liver and can cause the same metabolic issues as table sugar if you eat too much. Honey is basically glucose and fructose. It's not magic.

3

Elimination works because you eliminate everything

A lot of people come to carnivore because they have autoimmune issues, gut problems, or inflammation. Strict carnivore removes all potential triggers. Adding fruit back might reintroduce problems.

  • Very lean before starting ‚Äî If you don't have much body fat to burn, carbs can provide quick energy.

  • Insulin resistance or diabetes ‚Äî Carbs spike insulin. Zero-carb keeps insulin low and stable.
  • Significant weight to lose ‚Äî Zero-carb makes appetite control easier. Adding carbs can trigger cravings.
  • Gut issues (IBS, SIBO, Crohn's) ‚Äî Fiber and fructose can feed bad bacteria. Strict carnivore starves them out.

The Nuanced Take

Here's what I think is actually happening:

The debate isn't really about science. It's about individual response. Some people thrive on zero-carb. Some people do better with a little fruit. Both can work.

Paul Saladino is a super active guy who surfs and works out constantly. For him, carbs improve performance. That doesn't mean everyone needs them.

Shawn Baker is also super active (he's a former pro athlete). He thrives on zero-carb. That doesn't mean carbs are inherently bad.

Different people, different results.

How to Figure Out What Works for You

If you're new to carnivore, start strict. Meat, water, salt. Give it 90 days.

Why? Because you need to establish a baseline. You need to be fully fat-adapted before you can assess whether carbs help or hurt.

After 90 days, if you're feeling great, keep going. If you're struggling with energy, sleep, or hormones, you can experiment with adding carbs back in.

How to Test:

  • Add one source of carbs at a time. Honey first, or fruit. Don't add both at once.
  • Start small: 1-2 tablespoons of honey, or one piece of fruit per day.
  • Track how you feel: energy, sleep, digestion, skin, mood.
  • If it helps, keep it. If it causes problems, remove it again.

This is your body. You get to decide what works.

The Bottom Line

Paul Saladino isn't wrong. But he's not universally right either.

For some people, adding honey and fruit improves performance and well-being. For others, it reintroduces cravings, inflammation, and metabolic problems.

The community is split because both approaches work for different people.

If strict carnivore is working for you, you don't need to change. If you're struggling and suspect carbs might help, you can test it without abandoning the core principles: prioritize animal foods, avoid processed junk, listen to your body.

That's the real lesson here. There's no one perfect protocol. There's only what works for you.
