What Your Carnivore Bloodwork Actually Means

You start carnivore and your doctor wants bloodwork. You get the results. Total cholesterol is 280. Your LDL went up. Your doctor looks worried. Should you be?

Let me walk you through what's actually happening.

The Markers That Change

Glucose: Drops significantly (usually 20-40% decrease). This is good. Stable blood sugar is the point.

Triglycerides: Usually drop substantially. Low triglycerides = better metabolic health. Good sign.

Total Cholesterol: Often goes up. Might reach 280-350. Don't panic yet.

LDL Cholesterol: Usually increases. This is where doctors get concerned. But context matters.

HDL Cholesterol: Often increases (good cholesterol). This is positive.

Why LDL Goes Up (And Why It's Usually Fine)

On carnivore, your body is running on fat. You're producing more VLDL (very-low-density lipoprotein) to transport fat. More VLDL means higher LDL on the test.

But here's what doctors often miss: high LDL on carnivore is often large, fluffy LDL particles (less atherogenic). The test doesn't distinguish particle size.

LDL particle size test would show: your particles are likely good. But most doctors don't order it.

The Real Markers to Watch

Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio: This is more predictive than LDL alone. Calculate: triglycerides √∑ HDL. Want under 2 ideally. Most carnivore people get excellent ratios.

fasting glucose: Should drop significantly (good).

Inflammation markers (hsCRP): Should decrease (good).

LDL particle number (if tested): This matters more than LDL-C number. Most carnivore people have fewer particles (better cardiovascular health).

What Doctors Get Wrong

The standard lipid panel is 50 years old. It was designed for a carb-eating population. On carnivore, the same markers mean different things.

A doctor seeing LDL of 250 panics. But if your triglycerides are 60, your HDL is 90, your glucose is stable, and you're symptom-free‚Äîyou're probably fine.

When to Actually Worry

Worry if:

  • LDL is high AND triglycerides are high (sign of metabolic dysfunction)
  • Glucose is still elevated (diet not working)
  • HDL is low despite carnivore
  • Inflammation markers (hsCRP) don't improve
  • You have actual heart symptoms

The Conversation to Have With Your Doctor

"My LDL is 250, but my triglycerides dropped to 60, HDL increased to 95, and my glucose normalized. My Trig/HDL ratio is excellent. How concerned should I actually be?"

Good doctors will: check particle size, look at ratio markers, acknowledge context.

Poor doctors will: only see LDL number, recommend statins, miss the bigger picture.

Should You Get a Particle Size Test?

If your LDL is significantly elevated and your doctor is worried, yes. Tests like VAP, NMR lipoprotein panel, or Lp(a) testing clarify what's actually happening.

Most carnivore people who do this find: their particles are larger and fewer. That's favorable.

The Bloodwork Timeline

  • Baseline: Before carnivore (for comparison)
  • 8 weeks: First recheck. Most markers shifting.
  • 12 weeks: True picture of where you're settling. Good time to decide if diet is working.
  • 6 months: Stabilized state. If improvements happened, they're visible now.

Red Flags vs. False Alarms

Real red flag: High LDL + high triglycerides + low HDL + elevated glucose = metabolic dysfunction. This shouldn't happen on well-done carnivore.

False alarm: High LDL + low triglycerides + high HDL + low glucose = probably fine. Just unusual lipid profile.

Bottom Line

Get bloodwork. Track trends. But interpret it correctly. Don't panic at LDL alone. Look at the full picture: glucose, triglycerides, HDL, particle metrics, inflammation markers, symptoms.

Most people on carnivore show metabolic improvement. Your doctor might just need education on how to read it.
