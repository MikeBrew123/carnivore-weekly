What "Normal" Actually Means (And Why It's Misleading)

The official guidelines say fasting glucose under 100 mg/dL is "normal." But research suggests insulin resistance often starts developing when fasting glucose creeps above 85 mg/dL. That's a 15-point gap where your body might be struggling, but conventional medicine isn't flagging it yet.

Why Morning Glucose Rises (Even When You Don't Eat)

You might think, "I didn't eat anything overnight, so why isn't my glucose lower?" The answer is your liver.

The Dawn Phenomenon: What's Happening Inside

While you sleep, your liver releases stored glucose to keep your brain and body running. This is totally normal‚Äîit's called hepatic glucose output.

But when you're insulin resistant, your liver doesn't respond properly to insulin's signal to "stop making glucose." So it keeps pumping out more than you need, and your morning number climbs higher than it should.

This is why someone with insulin resistance can wake up with a fasting glucose of 110 mg/dL even after eating a perfect carnivore dinner the night before. It's not about what you ate‚Äîit's about how your metabolism is functioning at a cellular level.

What to Do If Your Numbers Show Insulin Resistance

If your fasting glucose sits above 90 mg/dL or your HOMA-IR is above 2.0, here's what actually works:

1

Prioritize Protein, Eliminate Processed Carbs

Non-negotiable. Insulin resistance improves dramatically when you remove foods that spike insulin repeatedly. Going full carnivore dropped my fasting glucose from 95 to 82 mg/dL in 8 weeks.

2

Track Fasting Insulin, Not Just Glucose

Ask your doctor to order a fasting insulin test alongside your glucose. It's a standard blood test‚Äîmost just don't suggest it automatically. You need both numbers for the full picture.

3

Retest in 8-12 Weeks

Insulin sensitivity doesn't improve overnight. Give your body time to adapt, then recheck your HOMA-IR score. You'll see fasting insulin drop first, then glucose follows.

"Insulin resistance isn't a life sentence. It's feedback. Your body is telling you something needs to change, and it will respond when you give it the right inputs."
