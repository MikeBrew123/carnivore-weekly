Do You Actually Need Supplements on Carnivore?

There's a subset of the carnivore community that sells supplements. They'll tell you that you need their electrolyte mix, their organ blend, their vitamin stack. And yes, supplements can help.

But do you need them? Honestly? No. Most people don't.

Let me break down what you actually need, what's optional, and what's pure marketing.

What You Probably Don't Need

Multivitamins: Carnivore is nutrient-dense. If you're eating organ meat even once weekly, you're covered on most micronutrients. Beef liver alone has more vitamin A, B vitamins, and iron than any multivitamin.

Protein powders: You're eating meat. You have protein. Unless you're a competitive athlete needing post-workout shake, you don't need powder.

Fat-soluble vitamin supplements: Meat has vitamin D, K2, E. Organs have them concentrated. You're covered without supplementing.

Most herbs and adaptogens: Mushrooms, medicinal herbs, whatever's trendy. They might help at the margins, but they're not essential. Marketing mostly.

What You Might Actually Need (Context-Dependent)

Salt

Not a supplement‚Äîit's seasoning. But you need MORE on carnivore than you think. At least 1 teaspoon daily, often more if you're active or in cold climate. This solves 80% of "I feel tired" problems.

Electrolytes (In Specific Scenarios)

You might need:

  • Heavy exercise during adaptation (first 4 weeks)
  • Very hot climate and sweating a lot
  • Leg cramps that persist despite adequate salt

How to address it: Salt food heavily first. Drink water with salt added. Then, if still needed, electrolyte supplementation (LMNT, True Salt, etc.) can help.

You probably don't need: Daily electrolyte supplements if you're sedentary, in moderate climate, and salting your food adequately.

Organ Meat If You Can't Source It

Organ meats are the most nutrient-dense food available. If you can get them, get them 1-2x weekly. If you can't access liver or kidney, an organ supplement (like Heart + Soil) can fill gaps.

But this is nice-to-have, not essential. Muscle meat alone sustains people fine.

When Supplementation Actually Makes Sense

Scenario 1: You have pre-existing deficiencies. If you came into carnivore with vitamin D deficiency or anemia, supplementing makes sense while your diet corrects it.

Scenario 2: You have specific health conditions. Thyroid issues might benefit from iodine. Certain autoimmune conditions might benefit from specific micronutrients. Work with a practitioner on this.

Scenario 3: You're an athlete with high demands. Electrolytes, maybe additional micronutrients, might help during heavy training. But this is optimization, not necessity.

Scenario 4: You're testing for nutritional deficiency. Get blood work. If something's actually low, supplement it. Don't guess.

The Salt Question (Most Important)

Carnivore flushes sodium and potassium faster than carb diets. This is biology, not opinion. When people feel tired, weak, or get cramps in weeks 2-3, it's usually salt.

Solution: salt your food generously. That's it. You don't need special electrolyte mixes if salt solves it.

How much salt? Aim for 1-2 teaspoons daily minimum. More if you're active or hot. Your body will tell you if you have enough (you'll feel good). If you're tired and weak, add salt.

The Organ Meat Question

Organ meats hit a different nutrient profile than muscle meat. If you can source them, eat them 1-2x weekly. If you can't:

  • Your health won't suffer without them
  • But you'll optimize faster if you include them
  • If supplementing is easier, organ blends work

Don't feel pressured to supplement organs if you can eat the real thing. Real food beats supplement every time.

The Business Reality

Supplement companies have financial incentive to make you think you need their products. This creates bias in marketing. Take supplement recommendations with that in mind.

Evaluate based on: Do I have actual evidence I'm deficient? Will this supplement address the actual problem? Or am I buying marketing?

My Honest Framework

  1. Salt adequately. Non-negotiable. Solve salt first.
  2. Eat organ meat if accessible. Once weekly minimum, twice weekly ideal.
  3. Get bloodwork if concerned. Testing beats guessing.
  4. Supplement only if deficient. Not "just in case."
  5. Re-test in 3 months. See if supplementing actually helped.

Bottom Line

Most people don't need supplements on carnivore. Salt your food. Eat organ meat if possible. Eat adequate muscle meat. That's sufficient for most people.

If you have specific health issues, pre-existing deficiencies, or are an athlete, supplementation makes sense. But the baseline? You're fine without it.

Don't buy supplements out of fear. Buy them because you've identified actual need.
